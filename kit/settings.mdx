#### Multi-factor authentication

This adds a layer of additional security to your Kit account, in case your login credentials are stolen. When switched on, you'll be challenged for a unique one-time code to enter to access your account.

<Frame>
  ![Image](/images/image-8.png)
</Frame>

There are two options you can switch on; Authenticator app and SMS message.

#### Authenticator app

When you enable this option, you'll be prompted to enter a one-time passcode which is generated by your preferred authenticator app. If you don't already have one, Google's **Authenticator** **app** is a good option, and it's free to use.

<Frame>
  ![Image](/images/image-9.png)
</Frame>

You'll find the app available in both the Apple Store and Google Play. To set it up, scan the QR code presented to you when you toggle the Authenticator app in your Kit account.

<Frame>
  ![Image](/images/image-10.png)
</Frame>

Once you successfully submit your unique six-digit number provided by your authenticator, you'll see this message pop up to confirm that you have enabled multi-factor authentication.

<Frame>
  ![Image](/images/image-11.png)
</Frame>

**SMS Authentication**

If you prefer to use SMS authentication, or add it to give more convenience by having two ways in which to authenticate your account, you can switch that option on too.

<Frame>
  ![Image](/images/image-12.png)
</Frame>

You'll be asked for the country code for your mobile phone and your phone number. When challenged, you need to enter the unique six-digit number sent to your mobile.

<Note>
  It's worth noting that SMS messaging is not as reliable as a messaging service if you are located in the United Arab Emirates (UAE) region. An authenticator app would be a more reliable option in this scenario.
</Note>

<Card title="John's recommendation" type="tip" horizontal>
  At a minimum, implement at least one form of multi-factor authentication, and I recommend using the Google Authenticator app (available on both iOS and Android). It's free and simple to use, and will give you peace of mind.
</Card>

#### Verified sending domains

Before we look at how to verify your sending domain, let's understand exactly what this means.

Most of the mailbox providers, like Gmail, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Apple, all look for certain signals to make sure the emails you send to their subscribers are genuinely from you (or, more accurately, from Kit, with your permission) and that you're applying best practices with your email marketing.

A verified sending domain requires three main records to be inserted into your DNS records with your domain host (e.g. GoDaddy, Hostinger, Ionos, etc.).

**The three DNS records are shown below;**

| Authentication | Name | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SPF | Sender Policy Framework | This lets the mailbox providers know who has permission to send emails on your behalf, e.g. Kit |
| DKIM | Domain Keys Identified Mail | This acts a digital signature, ensuring the email has not been tampered with between it being sent and being received. |
| DMARC | Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance | This determines what to do if either SPF or DKIM fails, with three options; do nothing, send to quarantine, or refuse the email. |

<Frame>
  ![Verified Sending Domains](/images/Verified-Sending-Domains.png)
</Frame>

In the image above, you can see there is already a verified sending domain listed, and the green tick to indicate that Kit has detected your DNS records have successfully been inserted.

The good news is that you don't need to be technical to be able to do this, as Kit provides a tool that will do it for you.

**Add a verified sending domain**

The cost to buy a domain will vary depending on which one you buy, but as a general guide, you can expect to pay \$20-\$50/year for one domain.

This is an option that displays a page to subscribers after they subscribe to your email list, requesting their consent for additional emails. This is to comply with GDPR (General Data Protection Regulations).

### Custom Domains Using Your Own Domain

In addition to the one Kit provides, you can use any other domain you own to create custom domains; for example I own [kitmastery.com](http://kitmastery.com), so I can create subdomains (or 'custom' domains) to use for my Kit assets.

To use your own domain, you need to insert a few entries into your DNS records. If you're not very techy and rely on someone else to take care of that for you, they can easily do it in a few minutes. 

That said, the task is literally copying and pasting values, so it's not a technical task - anyone can do it. 

**Example:**

I created a subdomain for my own domain and added it to my Kit account. The domain I won is **kitmastery.com** and the subdomain I chose is '**hub**' (that's the name of my community). So, together, my custom domain in Kit, using a domain I own, becomes **hub.kitmastery.com**.

The image below shows the the records that need to be copied from Kit (Kit supplies everything you need), and you then paste the values in to your DNS records. Your DNS records will be accessible via your domain host (e.g. GoDaddy, Hostinger, Ionos, etc.).
